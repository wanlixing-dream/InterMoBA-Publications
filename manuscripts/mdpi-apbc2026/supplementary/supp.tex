\section{Supplementary Methods}

\subsection{Detailed MoBA Mechanism}

\subsubsection{Dynamic Partition and Top-K Selection Details}

The MoBA mechanism dynamically allocates attention resources by computing an importance score for each node/edge and adjusting feature chunking via a Top-K selection strategy. The chunk size for each region is dynamically set based on an aggregated score of its node and edge features, distance, contact frequency, and centrality.

\textbf{Supplementary Eq. S1: Calculation of Dynamic Chunk Size.}
The chunk size for a batch region $b$ is determined as follows:
\begin{equation}
\text{ChunkSize}_b = \max(\text{MergedLen}_b, \text{BaseChunkSize})
\end{equation}
where $\text{BaseChunkSize}=32$ is the minimum granularity used in our experiments. $\text{MergedLen}_b$ is the length of the merged key region after fusing importance scores. The fusion score for a node $i$ is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
\text{FusionScore}_i = \text{MLP}_{\text{fuse}}\left(\left[ \text{Imp}_i, \text{ContactFreq}_i, \text{Centrality}_i \right]\right)
\end{equation}
Here, $\text{Imp}_i$ is the node importance score, $\text{ContactFreq}_i$ is the contact frequency, and $\text{Centrality}_i$ is a centrality measure. These scores are then normalized using softmax:
\begin{equation}
\text{SoftmaxScore}_i = \frac{\exp(\text{FusionScore}_i)}{\sum_j \exp(\text{FusionScore}_j)}
\end{equation}
The Top-K highest-scoring regions are selected for full-precision attention:
\begin{equation}
\text{Top-K} = \text{argsort}(\text{SoftmaxScore})[:k]
\end{equation}
where $k = \max(10, \lceil 0.05 \times N \rceil)$, and $N$ is the total number of nodes in the input graph.

\subsection{Inter-Blocks Computation Details}

In the main paper, the Inter-Blocks module is introduced to model cross-molecular interactions between protein and ligand atoms. Due to space constraints, the complete derivation of the attention update was omitted. Here we provide the full formulation that corresponds to Equation~(4) in the main text.

Given the updated node features $H$ from the previous Intra-Blocks (Equation~(5) in the main paper) and the edge feature transformation $E'$, the Inter-Blocks apply masked self-attention with a protein–ligand specific mask $M''$:

\begin{equation}
H^{\text{inter}} = \mathrm{MaskAttn}(H, E', M''),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{MaskAttn}$ is defined in Equation~(3) of the main paper. The mask $M''$ is defined element-wise as:
\begin{equation}
M''_{ij} = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } v_i \in V_L,\; v_j \in V_P \quad \text{(ligand-to-protein direction)},\\
1 & \text{if } v_i \in V_P,\; v_j \in V_L \quad \text{(protein-to-ligand direction)},\\
-\infty & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
This ensures that attention is only computed between ligand and protein nodes, fully capturing bidirectional intermolecular interactions while ignoring intra-molecular pairs.

The edge feature transformation $E'$ is obtained by combining the original distance-based bias $A'$ with a residual update:
\begin{equation}
E' = A' + \mathrm{FFN}\bigl(\mathrm{LN}(d)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $A'$ is the distance‑based attention bias (derived from interatomic distances), $d$ denotes the pairwise distance matrix, $\mathrm{LN}$ is layer normalization, and $\mathrm{FFN}$ is a two‑layer feed‑forward network. This formulation corresponds exactly to the missing Equation~(4) in the main paper and completes the description of the Inter-Blocks module.

\subsection{Training Hyperparameters}
The model was trained using the AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of $1\times10^{-4}$, a weight decay of $1\times10^{-2}$, and a batch size of 32. We employed a cosine annealing learning rate scheduler with a warm-up period of 5000 steps. Mixed-precision training (fp16) was used to accelerate computation and reduce memory footprint. Models were trained on a single NVIDIA RTX 3080 GPU for a maximum of 200 epochs, with early stopping based on the validation loss.

\subsection{Dataset Split Details}
For the time-split PDBBind benchmark, we followed the protocol established in previous works (e.g., DiffDock, Interformer). Specifically, we used the PDBBind v2016 dataset and split complexes based on their PDB release year, ensuring that all test set complexes have a release year later than 2016, while training and validation sets contain older entries. This yields 333 complexes in the test set. The full (random) split is also used for ablation studies.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Sampling Procedure}
InterMoBA generates candidate ligand poses via a Monte Carlo (MC) sampling algorithm. Starting from an initial random orientation and torsion angles of the ligand within the binding pocket, the MC simulation performs 5000 steps. At each step, a random translation, rotation, or torsion change is proposed, and the energy (negative log-likelihood of the mixture density function) is evaluated. The proposal is accepted according to the Metropolis criterion. The lowest-energy conformations among the sampled trajectory are retained as the Top-K poses (K=10 by default).

\subsection{Analysis of Top-K Selection Ratio on RMSD and Interaction Recovery}

\textbf{Supplementary Table S2: Theoretical implications of the current Top-K selection ratio.}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{\textbf{Theoretical implications of the current Top-K selection ratio.}}
\begin{tabular}{lp{8cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value / Implication} \\
\midrule
Selection ratio $k/N$ & $\approx 5\%$ (from $k = \max(10, 0.05N)$) \\
Fraction processed with reduced precision & $>95\%$ \\
Observed H-bond recovery & $63.74\%$ (high) \\
Observed hydrophobic recovery & $54.69\%$ (high) \\
Observed Top-1 RMSD gain over Interformer & $+1.6$ percentage points (modest) \\
Observed PB-valid gap & Visible (Supp. Fig. 6), consistent with limited global information \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Since the selection ratio is fixed in the current implementation, we provide here a theoretical analysis of its implications. The 5\% retention ratio implies that over 95\% of protein-ligand atom pairs are processed with reduced attention precision. While this strategy effectively focuses computational resources on regions with high importance scores, as reflected in the high interaction recovery rates for hydrogen bonds (63.74\%) and hydrophobic contacts (54.69\%), it also inherently limits the propagation of global structural information. This limitation is consistent with the observation that the Top-1 RMSD success rate (65.5\%) exceeds Interformer (63.9\%) by only 1.6 percentage points, a modest gain compared to the improvement in interaction recovery.

\textbf{Supplementary Note 1: Relationship between sparse attention and PoseBusters PB-valid gap.}

The atomic distance violations reported in Supplementary Figure 6 provide indirect evidence of the global information limitation. Specifically, the absence of long range constraints in the attention mechanism allows the model to produce poses that satisfy local interaction patterns but may violate steric boundaries between non adjacent atoms. A quantitative sensitivity analysis of the selection ratio, for example varying $k$ from 1\% to 20\% of $N$, would be required to establish a causal relationship. We leave this investigation for future work.

\subsection{Analysis of Physical Plausibility and PoseBusters Results}

\textbf{Supplementary Figure 6 (expanded caption):} The figure compares RMSD based success rates (dark bars) with PB-valid rates (light bars) for InterMoBA and baseline methods. The gap between the two bars indicates the fraction of poses that achieve RMSD $<$ 2\,\AA\ but violate at least one PoseBusters validity criterion. According to the PoseBusters report, the most frequent violation for deep learning based docking methods is atomic distance clashes between ligand and protein atoms. InterMoBA exhibits a similar pattern, suggesting that the MDN based energy function, which is trained to maximize the likelihood of observed interatomic distances without a penalty for van der Waals overlaps, does not inherently prevent steric clashes.

\textbf{Supplementary Note 2: Causes and potential remedies for atomic distance violations.}

\noindent \textit{Causes:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The training loss (Equation 11 in the main text) maximizes the conditional probability density of distances but does not include a repulsive term for distances below the sum of van der Waals radii.
    \item The Monte Carlo sampling procedure (5000 steps) uses the negative log likelihood as the energy function; without a clash penalty, low energy poses may still contain penetrations.
    \item The Top-K sparse attention reduces information exchange across distant atom pairs, which may weaken the model's ability to enforce global steric constraints.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textit{Potential remedies (not implemented in this work):}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Augment the MDN loss with a steric clash penalty term:
    $L_{\text{total}} = L_{\text{MDN}} + \lambda \sum_{i,j} \max(0, r_{\text{vdW}} - d_{ij})^2$.
    \item Increase the Top-K selection ratio, for example to 10\% or 20\% of $N$, to improve global information flow at the cost of computational efficiency.
    \item Apply post hoc energy minimization using a classical force field such as MMFF94 to resolve clashes while preserving the interaction pattern.
\end{enumerate}
